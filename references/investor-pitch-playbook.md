# Investor Pitch Playbook

A comprehensive reference for pitching video games to investors, publishers, and funders — especially those who don't come from gaming backgrounds. This document covers market intelligence frameworks, investor psychology, jargon translation, competitive analysis, persuasion strategy, and pitch deck architecture.

Read this file before generating any investor-facing content: pitch decks, investor memos, competitive analyses, market sizing, fundraising strategies, or publisher presentations.

## 1. Understanding Your Audience

Games are pitched to fundamentally different investor archetypes. Each thinks differently, cares about different metrics, and speaks a different language. Misreading your audience is the fastest way to lose a deal.

### Investor Archetypes

**Venture Capitalists (VCs)**
VCs are looking for outsized returns — 10x or more. They evaluate games the way they evaluate startups: team, market, traction, scalability. Most VCs have limited gaming knowledge. They understand SaaS metrics (CAC, LTV, retention, churn) better than they understand game design. Your job is to frame your game in their existing mental models.

What VCs care about: market size, team pedigree, defensible IP, scalability (can this become a franchise, a platform, a service?), comparable exits and returns, speed to revenue.

What VCs don't care about (but you do): artistic vision, narrative depth, critical acclaim potential — unless you can translate these into business outcomes (earned media, long-tail sales, franchise value).

The VC funding landscape has contracted significantly. Gaming-related startups raised roughly $627M in H1 2025 — far below the $2.82B raised in 2023. This means VCs are pickier, and your pitch must be tighter. The silver lining: if you get funded, you have less competition for attention.

**Publishers**
Publishers understand games. They evaluate playability, market fit, production risk, and portfolio fit. They want to see a playable build or at minimum a vertical slice. Publisher pitches are more about demonstrating craft and production capability than about market sizing.

What publishers care about: gameplay quality, production feasibility, team's ability to ship, genre/market fit within their portfolio, marketing hooks, platform strategy.

**Angel Investors / Individual Investors**
Often successful people who love games. More likely to invest emotionally — they want to fund something they'd personally play. They respond to passion, vision, and creative ambition more than spreadsheets. But they still need to believe their money will return.

**Private Equity**
PE has become extremely active in gaming, but primarily for late-stage, profitable studios. If you're pre-revenue, PE is rarely the right target. PE cares about EBITDA, recurring revenue, and operational efficiency.

**Grants and Government Funding**
Many countries and regions offer game development grants. These require demonstrating cultural value, job creation, and regional economic impact alongside commercial viability. Frame your game as both art and economic engine.

## 2. The Jargon Translation Engine

The single biggest barrier between game developers and investors is language. Investors hear game jargon and their eyes glaze over — not because they're stupid, but because they lack the referent experiences. Your job is translation. Every game concept has a business or technology analogue that investors already understand.

### Genre Translation Table

| Game Term | Investor Translation | Why This Works |
|-----------|---------------------|----------------|
| Roguelike | "Procedurally generated content — think Netflix's recommendation algorithm, but for gameplay. Each session is unique, which drives replay value and engagement." | Maps to personalization and engagement metrics investors understand from tech. |
| Metroidvania | "A gated exploration model — like a freemium SaaS product where unlocking capabilities opens new areas of value. Players see content they can't access yet, which drives motivation to progress." | Maps to conversion funnels and feature gating. |
| Souls-like | "Premium difficulty-as-a-feature — like Peloton positioning hard workouts as aspirational. The challenge IS the product. It creates intense community engagement and social proof through shared achievement." | Maps to community-driven growth and aspirational branding. |
| Open World | "A content platform with hundreds of hours of engagement per user. Think of it as a theme park — multiple attractions, non-linear exploration, high time-spent." | Maps to engagement metrics and platform thinking. |
| Battle Royale | "100-player competitive format with viral spectator appeal — like a tournament sport with built-in streaming/broadcast value." | Maps to network effects and media rights. |
| RPG | "Deep character progression system that keeps players engaged for 40-100+ hours. The investment players make in their character creates switching costs — once you're 50 hours in, you don't leave." | Maps to retention and switching costs. |
| Narrative Adventure | "Story-driven experience with strong IP potential — think prestige TV for gaming. Lower development cost, high critical acclaim potential, strong franchise/adaptation value." | Maps to IP value and content licensing. |
| Sandbox / Survival Craft | "User-generated content platform where players build the engagement loop themselves. Minecraft model — low content creation cost, near-infinite replay value, massive community." | Maps to UGC platforms and community moats. |
| Co-op / Multiplayer | "Social experience with built-in viral acquisition — every player recruits friends. Social games benefit from network effects that single-player titles don't." | Maps to viral coefficients and network effects. |
| Live Service | "Games-as-a-Service (GaaS) — recurring revenue model with regular content updates, seasonal passes, and cosmetic monetization. Think SaaS but for entertainment." | Maps directly to SaaS/recurring revenue models. |
| Early Access | "Paid beta — players fund development while providing real-time feedback. It's customer-funded R&D with built-in product-market fit validation." | Maps to lean startup methodology and customer development. |
| Indie Game | "Boutique studio model — small team, low overhead, high creative focus. Like an independent film: lower budget, higher ROI potential if it connects with its audience." | Maps to capital efficiency and indie film economics. |

### Mechanic-to-Business Translation

| Game Mechanic | Business Equivalent |
|--------------|---------------------|
| Player choice / branching narrative | Personalized user experience — different value for every customer |
| Procedural generation | Algorithmic content creation — scales without proportional cost increase |
| Mod support | User-generated content ecosystem — community extends the product for free |
| Seasonal content updates | Recurring engagement hooks — same model as streaming platforms releasing new seasons |
| Difficulty settings | Market segmentation — accessibility broadens the addressable audience |
| Speedrunning community | Organic competitive esports — free engagement content and community marketing |
| Achievement systems | Gamified retention mechanics — the same psychology used by fitness apps and loyalty programs |
| Cross-platform play | Platform-agnostic distribution — maximizes addressable market |
| Leaderboards | Competitive social mechanics — drives retention through social comparison (same psychology behind LinkedIn) |

### Metric Translation

| Game Metric | Investor Metric | What It Means |
|------------|----------------|---------------|
| DAU/MAU | Daily/Monthly Active Users | Same as tech — engagement and stickiness |
| Session length | Time-on-platform | How captivating the experience is |
| D1/D7/D30 retention | Day-1/7/30 retention | Same as mobile apps — how well the product hooks users |
| ARPU/ARPPU | Average Revenue Per User / Per Paying User | Same as SaaS/mobile — monetization efficiency |
| Wishlists (Steam) | Pre-orders / intent signals | Leading indicator of demand — median conversion ~15-27% to first-month sales |
| Steam reviews (% positive) | Net Promoter Score equivalent | Product quality and word-of-mouth indicator |
| Concurrent players | Peak engagement | Demonstrates market demand and server infrastructure needs |
| Median playtime | Product engagement depth | How much value users extract from the product |
| Refund rate | Churn / product-market fit indicator | Steam's 2-hour refund window is essentially a free trial |

## 3. Market Intelligence Frameworks

Investors need to see that you understand your market — not just creatively, but commercially. Here's how to research, structure, and present market data convincingly.

### Where to Find Reliable Market Data

**Free / Low-Cost Sources:**
- **Newzoo** — Global games market reports (some free summaries, full reports paid). The industry-standard source for overall market sizing.
- **Sensor Tower / Video Game Insights (VGI)** — Steam-specific data: genre performance, revenue estimates, player counts, tag analysis.
- **SteamDB** — Real-time Steam statistics, concurrent player data, historical pricing.
- **Steam Spy** — Ownership and player estimates (less accurate post-privacy changes, but still directional).
- **GamesIndustry.biz** — Industry news with revenue and deal data.
- **Game Developer (formerly Gamasutra)** — Postmortems, market analysis, developer surveys.
- **Newzoo / Niko Partners** — Regional market data, especially Asia-Pacific.
- **IGDA Developer Satisfaction Survey** — Industry benchmarks on team sizes, budgets, development timelines.

**Paid Sources (for serious pitches):**
- **Newzoo Pro** — Detailed market segmentation.
- **Statista** — Cross-industry data, useful for framing games within broader entertainment.
- **Sensor Tower** — Mobile-specific market intelligence.

### TAM/SAM/SOM Framework for Games

Every investor expects a TAM/SAM/SOM slide. Here's how to build one that's credible rather than aspirational fiction.

**TAM (Total Addressable Market):**
The entire market that could theoretically buy your product if you had infinite resources and reach. For games, this is typically the global revenue of your target platform(s).
- Framework: "The global [platform] gaming market generates $[X]B annually."
- Be honest about what's included. Don't claim the entire $205B global market if you're making a PC-only indie RPG.

**SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market):**
The segment of the TAM that your game actually targets — filtered by genre, platform, geography, price point, and audience demographics.
- Framework: "Within [platform], [genre] games generated $[X]B in [year]. Our target audience — [demographic/psychographic description] — represents approximately [X]% of this segment."
- Use genre-level data from VGI, Newzoo, or Sensor Tower.

**SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market):**
The realistic slice you can capture in years 1-3, given your marketing budget, team size, and competitive landscape.
- Framework: "Based on comparable title performance (see competitive analysis), conservative first-year revenue projection is $[X]M, representing [X]% of the SAM."
- Anchor to comparable titles. "Games similar to ours in scope and genre have sold between [X] and [Y] units in their first year."

### Genre-Level Market Analysis Framework

When presenting genre-specific data, structure it like this:

1. **Genre definition and scope** — Define exactly what you mean. "Action RPG" is broad. "Real-time action RPG with soulslike combat and narrative focus" is specific enough to analyze.
2. **Revenue share on target platform** — What percentage of platform revenue does this genre represent?
3. **Growth trajectory** — Is this genre growing, stable, or declining? Use 3-5 year trend data.
4. **Competition density** — How many titles released in this genre last year? Is the market oversaturated or underserved?
5. **Breakout potential** — What recent titles exceeded expectations? Why?
6. **Audience demographics** — Who plays these games? Age, geography, spending patterns.

### Regional Market Framework

For games targeting specific regions or going global:

- **Asia-Pacific:** ~46% of global revenue. Mobile-dominant. China requires regulatory approval (ISBN license). Japan/Korea have distinct preferences.
- **North America:** ~24% of global revenue. Console and PC strong. Highest ARPU.
- **Europe:** ~19% of global revenue. Diverse preferences by country. Strong indie scene in Nordics and UK.
- **Latin America:** Growing market, price-sensitive. Localization into Portuguese and Spanish pays dividends.
- **Middle East & Africa:** Fastest-growing region by player count, but lower ARPU.

## 4. Competitive Analysis Methodology

Never pitch in a vacuum. A strong competitive analysis demonstrates market awareness and positions your game's differentiation.

### The Four-Quadrant Comparable Framework

For every pitch, identify four categories of comparable titles:

**1. Direct Comparables**
Games in the same genre, scale, and price bracket that your game will be directly compared to in the market.
- What to research: units sold, revenue, budget estimate, team size, development time, review scores, peak concurrent players.
- How to present: "Our direct comparable set includes [Title A] ($[X]M revenue, [X]-person team), [Title B] ($[X]M revenue), and [Title C] ($[X]M revenue). The median first-year revenue for this set is $[X]M."

**2. Aspiration Comparables**
The ceiling cases — breakout hits in your genre that redefined expectations. These show investors what success looks like at scale.
- How to present: "The upside case is demonstrated by titles like [Breakout Title], which earned $[X]M with a team of [X]. Our game shares [specific similarity] while targeting [specific underserved angle]."

**3. Anti-Comparables (Failure Analysis)**
Games that attempted something similar and underperformed. This is counterintuitive — why show failures? — but investors deeply respect founders who understand why things fail.
- How to present: "We studied [Failed Title], which underperformed despite [its strengths]. The key failure points were [specific reasons]. Our approach differs in [specific ways]."

**4. Adjacent Market Comparables**
Successful games in neighboring genres whose audiences overlap with yours. This expands the perceived market without making outlandish TAM claims.
- How to present: "[Adjacent Title] proved that audiences who enjoy [shared quality] will pay $[X] for [type of experience]. Our game captures this audience while adding [unique element]."

### Differentiation Matrix

Create a comparison matrix showing how your game differentiates:

| Feature | Your Game | Comparable A | Comparable B | Comparable C |
|---------|-----------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Core mechanic | [Unique aspect] | [Their approach] | [Their approach] | [Their approach] |
| Narrative depth | [Your strength] | [Their level] | [Their level] | [Their level] |
| Art style | [Distinctive] | [Common] | [Common] | [Common] |
| Price point | [Yours] | [Theirs] | [Theirs] | [Theirs] |
| Content hours | [Yours] | [Theirs] | [Theirs] | [Theirs] |

The matrix should make your differentiation visually obvious. Don't cherry-pick — include categories where comparables beat you, then explain why your choices are deliberate.

### How to Find Revenue Data for Comparables

- **Steam**: Use VGI (vginsights.com) or Gamalytic for estimated revenue. Cross-reference with SteamDB for player counts. Remember: Steam revenue estimates are gross — subtract 30% for Valve's cut, and note that some developers have negotiated better rates above $10M or $50M.
- **Console**: Less transparent. Use NPD/Circana data when available, press releases, and investor reports from publicly traded publishers.
- **Mobile**: Sensor Tower and data.ai provide estimated revenue.
- **Industry reports**: GDC postmortems, developer interviews, and financial disclosures from publicly traded studios.

## 5. Investor Psychology and Persuasion

Understanding how investors make decisions is as important as the content of your pitch. Investors don't make decisions purely on logic — they decide emotionally and justify rationally. Here's how to work with investor psychology rather than against it.

### The Investor Decision Framework

Most investors evaluate opportunities through five mental filters, usually in this order:

1. **Pattern matching** — "Does this look like things I've seen succeed?" Investors have a mental library of successful investments. Your job is to activate positive pattern matches while showing what's genuinely new.

2. **Risk assessment** — "What could go wrong, and do they know it?" Investors are more impressed by founders who articulate risks honestly than by founders who pretend risks don't exist. Acknowledge the elephants.

3. **Team conviction** — "Do I believe these people can pull this off?" At early stages, investors are betting on people more than products. Show shipped work, relevant expertise, and the team's unfair advantage for THIS specific game.

4. **Market timing** — "Is the window open?" Even great games can launch into bad timing. Show awareness of market cycles, platform trends, and cultural moments.

5. **Return potential** — "What does my upside look like?" Paint the ceiling, not just the floor. What happens if this game overperforms?

### The Persuasion Toolkit

**Anchoring**
The first number an investor hears becomes their reference point. Lead with strong anchors.
- "Hades sold 5M+ units with a team of 20. We're building a comparable scope with 15 people and a more underserved genre."
- "The indie RPG that most closely matches our profile — Disco Elysium — earned $40M+ on a $5M budget."

**Social Proof**
Investors follow other investors and validated signals. Use every form of social proof you have:
- Steam wishlists ("We have [X] wishlists organically, pre-marketing")
- Festival selections, awards, press coverage
- Community size (Discord members, social media followers)
- Previous investors or advisors with gaming expertise
- Notable developer pedigree ("Our lead designer shipped [Recognized Title]")

**Scarcity and Urgency**
Without being manipulative, create legitimate urgency:
- "Our round is [X]% committed — we're looking for [one/two] more strategic investors."
- "We're launching Early Access in [timeline] and want to close this round before announcement for maximum signal."

**The Narrative Arc**
Structure your pitch as a story with a beginning (the market opportunity), middle (your game's unique answer), and climax (the financial return). Investors remember stories far better than spreadsheets.

**Concrete Imagery**
When describing gameplay to non-gamers, use vivid, specific imagery rather than abstract descriptions.
- Bad: "It's an action RPG with deep narrative."
- Good: "Imagine you're a plague doctor in Renaissance Florence. Every patient you treat teaches you about a conspiracy in the Medici court. Your medical instruments double as weapons. The sicker the city gets, the more powerful — and more morally compromised — you become."

### Reading the Room

**Signs of engagement:** Leaning forward, asking detailed questions about the market or financials, introducing you to partners, asking about timeline and terms.

**Signs of polite disengagement:** Generic praise ("this is really interesting"), asking only surface questions, checking phone, redirecting to portfolio companies that "might be a better fit."

**The "wife test"**: Many experienced game investors apply what's informally called the "wife test" or "partner test" — can they explain your game to someone who doesn't play games, in one sentence, and make it sound exciting? If not, your pitch lacks a clear hook.

### Handling the "Games Are Risky" Objection

Every game investor conversation will include some version of "games are hit-driven and risky." This is true. Don't deny it. Instead, reframe:

- "You're right — games are hit-driven. But the hit rate improves dramatically with specific factors: experienced team, validated genre, pre-launch demand signals, and scope discipline. We have all four."
- "The risk profile of games is comparable to film, which has a well-established investment ecosystem. What makes games better: they have longer revenue tails, lower distribution costs, and the ability to course-correct in development through playtesting."
- "The median indie game earns very little — but the median game from a team that's shipped before earns substantially more. A team's third game averages roughly $209K in gross revenue, compared to $120K for a debut. We've shipped [X] games."

## 6. Pitch Deck Architecture

A game pitch deck to investors is NOT a GDD. It is a business case with a creative soul. The deck should be scannable in 3 minutes and deep enough for a 30-minute presentation.

### The 14-Slide Framework

**Slide 1: Cover**
- Game title, key art, studio name, one-line hook
- The hook should pass the "cocktail party test" — would someone at a party say "tell me more"?
- Example hook: "What if your medical diagnosis was the combat system?"

**Slide 2: The Opportunity**
- One slide, one thesis: "Here's the gap in the market and why it matters now."
- Lead with the market, not the game. Investors care about opportunities, not features.
- Example: "The narrative RPG market has grown 34% in 3 years, but 90% of titles still target the same Western fantasy setting. 2.4 billion gamers across Asia, Latin America, and Africa see almost zero representation of their cultures in premium RPGs."

**Slide 3: The Game**
- What the player DOES (not lore, not backstory — verbs)
- 2-3 core gameplay pillars described in plain language
- Embedded gameplay trailer or GIF if possible (30 seconds max)
- Use the genre translation table — no unexplained jargon

**Slide 4: Why This Game, Why Now**
- Market timing argument: genre trends, cultural moment, platform opportunity
- "The success of [Recent Hit] proved audience appetite for [quality your game shares]. We take this further by [differentiation]."

**Slide 5: The Market**
- TAM/SAM/SOM with credible sources
- Genre growth data
- Audience demographics
- Keep this one slide — investors want confidence you know the numbers, not a dissertation

**Slide 6: Competitive Landscape**
- Differentiation matrix (see Section 4)
- 4-6 comparable titles with revenue figures
- Your unique position mapped visually (2x2 matrix works well)
- Be honest about strong competitors — then show your angle

**Slide 7: Traction**
- Everything you have: prototype, vertical slice, Steam wishlists, press coverage, festival selections, community metrics, influencer interest
- This slide matters more than almost any other. Traction de-risks the investment.
- If you have a playable demo, say so — and offer it

**Slide 8: The Team**
- Shipped titles and relevant expertise
- Why THIS team for THIS game — the specific unfair advantage
- Key hires planned with funding
- Advisory board (if you have notable advisors)

**Slide 9: Business Model**
- Revenue streams: premium sales, DLC, cosmetics, battle passes, etc.
- Pricing strategy and rationale
- DLC/expansion roadmap (shows long-term revenue potential)
- Platform strategy (Steam, console, Epic, etc.)

**Slide 10: Go-to-Market**
- Community building strategy (Discord, social media, content creators)
- Marketing timeline aligned with development milestones
- Wishlist/demo strategy
- Festival and event plan
- Influencer and press strategy

**Slide 11: Financial Projections**
- Revenue model with three scenarios: conservative, base, optimistic
- Break-even analysis
- Unit economics (development cost per content-hour, marketing cost per wishlist)
- 3-5 year projection
- Be conservative in your base case — investors will mentally discount whatever you show them

**Slide 12: The Ask**
- How much you're raising
- What the money buys (specific milestones: "vertical slice → Early Access → 1.0 launch")
- Valuation / terms (if appropriate at this stage)
- Runway: how long this funding lasts
- What triggers the next raise (if applicable)

**Slide 13: Risk & Mitigation**
- 3-4 honest risks (scope creep, market timing, team retention, technology)
- Specific mitigation for each
- Scope ladder: "If we need to cut, here's what we keep and what we drop, and the game still works"
- Investors deeply respect this slide. It shows maturity.

**Slide 14: Vision**
- Where this goes after launch
- Franchise potential, sequel vision, transmedia (if credible)
- Community and modding ecosystem
- End on inspiration — remind them why this game deserves to exist

### Visual Design Principles for Game Pitch Decks

- **Key art carries the deck.** Invest in concept art and key art before the pitch. One stunning image is worth 10 slides of text.
- **Minimal text per slide.** The deck supports your verbal pitch — it doesn't replace it. Max 30 words per slide body.
- **Gameplay over concept art.** If you have gameplay footage, use it. A moving prototype beats a beautiful painting.
- **Consistent visual identity.** The deck should feel like it comes from the same world as the game.
- **Data visualization over data tables.** Charts > spreadsheets. Make the numbers visual.

### Generating Actual .pptx Pitch Decks

When the user wants a produced pitch deck (not just content/structure), you can generate a .pptx file. Suggest this as an option: "I can outline the content for each slide, or if you'd like, I can generate an actual .pptx pitch deck file for you. The generated deck will have clean layout and structure — you can then add your key art and gameplay footage."

When generating .pptx, follow these principles:
- Professional, clean layouts — not flashy
- Leave clear placeholders for key art and gameplay screenshots/video
- Use the game's color palette if known
- Include speaker notes with talking points for each slide
- Data slides should have chart placeholders with the data pre-filled

## 7. Financial Modeling for Games

### Revenue Projection Framework

**Unit Sales Estimation:**
1. Start with comparable titles' first-year unit sales
2. Apply adjustments: your marketing budget vs. theirs, your team's track record, current market conditions, genre trend direction
3. Model three scenarios with a multiplier: Conservative (0.5x median comparable), Base (1x median comparable), Optimistic (2x median comparable)

**Revenue Calculation:**
- Gross revenue = Units × Price
- Net revenue = Gross - Platform cut (typically 30%, declining to 25%/20% at higher tiers on Steam) - Returns/refunds (typically 5-10%)
- Include DLC/expansion revenue in year 2+ projections

**Key Metrics to Model:**
- Development cost (total and per-month burn rate)
- Marketing budget (typically 30-50% of development cost for indie, higher for AA/AAA)
- Break-even units = Total cost / (Net revenue per unit)
- ROI = (Net revenue - Total cost) / Total cost
- Time to break-even

### Budget Benchmarks (Framework)

Development costs vary enormously, but here are frameworks for research:
- **Solo developer / micro-indie:** $10K-$100K, 1-3 years
- **Small indie (2-5 people):** $100K-$500K, 1-3 years
- **Mid-size indie (5-15 people):** $500K-$3M, 2-4 years
- **AA studio (15-50 people):** $3M-$20M, 2-4 years
- **AAA studio (100+ people):** $50M-$300M+, 3-7 years

When presenting budgets to investors, break down into: personnel, technology/tools, art outsourcing, audio/music, QA, marketing, contingency (10-20%).

### The Wishlist-to-Revenue Pipeline

Steam wishlists are the strongest pre-launch demand signal. Here's the framework:
- **Wishlist-to-first-month-sales conversion:** Median ~15-27% depending on genre and marketing
- **First-month-to-lifetime multiplier:** Typically 3-5x first-month sales over the game's lifetime for well-maintained titles
- **Price sensitivity:** Every $5 increase above $20 reduces conversion but increases revenue per unit — model the elasticity

Present wishlist data to investors as validated demand: "We have [X] wishlists, which at median conversion rates projects [Y] first-month unit sales, or $[Z] in first-month gross revenue."

## 8. Common Objections and Responses

### "Games are too risky / hit-driven"

**Reframe:** "The risk profile is real but manageable. Here's how we mitigate it: [scope ladder], [vertical slice validation], [milestone-based funding], [experienced team], [pre-launch demand signals]. The top 8.5% of Steam releases in 2025 earned over $100K — and the single biggest predictor of reaching that threshold is team experience, which we have."

### "The market is oversaturated"

**Reframe:** "Steam released ~18,000+ games last year, but most are low-quality shovelware. The market for well-crafted, differentiated games in our genre is actually underserved. [Comparable breakout] proved this when it earned $[X]M in a 'crowded' genre — because it offered something genuinely different. That's our strategy."

### "How is this different from [Popular Title]?"

**Reframe this as a feature, not a threat:** "That comparison is encouraging — it validates the market appetite. Here's how we differentiate: [specific differences]. We're not competing for the same audience — we're expanding it by reaching [underserved segment]."

### "What if it takes longer / costs more than projected?"

**Show the scope ladder:** "We've designed the game in concentric rings of scope. The core experience works at -30% budget. Full scope adds [features]. If we need to cut, we cut from the outside in — the game ships either way, and the core is what makes it special."

### "I don't understand the game / genre"

**Don't explain more — translate better:** Use the jargon translation table. Show gameplay video. Describe the emotional experience, not the mechanics. "You know that feeling when you're binge-watching a show and you realize you care about the characters? Our game creates that feeling, but you're making the decisions."

### "What's the exit?"

**Be realistic:** "The primary return path is revenue — this is a profitable product, not a money-losing startup seeking acquisition. That said, successful game IP is acquisition-attractive (see: [recent acquisition examples in your genre]). Additional exit paths include: franchise value, licensing deals, and studio acquisition by a larger publisher."

### "Your projections seem aggressive / conservative"

**If aggressive:** "These are our optimistic scenario. Our base case is [lower number], which still returns [X]x. We'd rather show you the range than pretend certainty."
**If conservative:** "We deliberately modeled conservatively. The upside case, based on [comparable breakout], is [higher number]. We'd rather exceed projections than miss them."

## 9. Platform Economics Reference

Understanding platform economics helps you answer investor questions about margins and distribution.

### Framework for Researching Platform Economics

When pitching, know these numbers for your target platforms:

**PC (Steam):**
- Revenue share: 30% to Valve (drops to 25% after $10M, 20% after $50M)
- No upfront certification cost
- Refund policy: 2 hours / 14 days (model 5-10% refund rate)
- Steam Direct fee: $100 per title (recoupable)
- Wishlists are the primary demand signal — track and present these
- Regional pricing flexibility (important for global reach)

**Console (PlayStation / Xbox / Nintendo):**
- Revenue share: typically 30%
- Certification/submission costs and timelines
- Development kit costs
- First-party deal potential (exclusivity, marketing support)
- Physical retail adds manufacturing and distribution costs

**Epic Games Store:**
- Revenue share: 12% (significantly better than Steam)
- Exclusivity deals: upfront minimum guarantee in exchange for timed exclusivity
- Smaller audience but better per-unit economics

**Mobile (iOS / Android):**
- Revenue share: 30% (15% under $1M/year on both platforms)
- Discovery is the primary challenge — UA (user acquisition) costs are high
- Different monetization norms (F2P, ads, IAP)

### Key Economic Metrics for Investors

- **Gross margin:** Revenue minus platform cut, refunds, and payment processing
- **Net margin:** Gross margin minus development cost amortization, marketing, and ongoing operations
- **LTV:CAC ratio:** Lifetime value of a player vs. cost to acquire them (especially important for F2P/live service)
- **Revenue per employee:** Useful benchmark for studio efficiency

## 10. The Post-Pitch Playbook

The pitch meeting is the beginning, not the end. Here's what happens after.

### Follow-Up Protocol

- **Within 24 hours:** Send a thank-you email with the deck attached (PDF, not editable), any promised follow-up materials, and a clear next step.
- **Within 1 week:** If no response, follow up once with a brief update (new milestone, wishlist growth, press mention — something that adds value, not just "checking in").
- **Ongoing:** Add interested investors to your monthly/quarterly update email. Consistent progress updates are the best fundraising tool.

### The Update Email

Send monthly or quarterly updates to all investor contacts:
- Progress: milestones hit, development progress, team growth
- Traction: wishlist growth, community metrics, press/festival updates
- Numbers: budget status, runway remaining
- Ask: what you need next (not always money — introductions, advice, playtest feedback)

### Building Investor Relationships Before You Need Money

The best time to pitch is when you don't need money yet. Build relationships 6-12 months before your raise:
- Attend GDC, game investment events, and demo showcases
- Share development updates publicly — investors follow studios before investing
- Ask for advice, not money — investors who advise often invest later
- Build a network of other founders who can make warm introductions

### When to Walk Away

Not every investor is the right investor. Walk away if:
- They want creative control without understanding games
- The terms are exploitative (taking >30% equity for a seed round, for example)
- They have no gaming portfolio or gaming understanding and aren't willing to learn
- They pressure you to change the game's creative direction to reduce "risk"
- The chemistry is wrong — you'll be working with this person for years
