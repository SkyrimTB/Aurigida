## 1. What Taste Is (and Isn't)

Taste is not preference. Preference is "I like dark fantasy." Taste is "I know why *this* dark fantasy concept will haunt players and *that* one will feel generic, and I can articulate the structural reasons for the difference."

Taste is not elitism. It's not about highbrow vs. lowbrow — Miyazaki drew from Berserk manga AND classical European painting AND Grimm's fairy tales. Fumito Ueda pulled from Chirico's metaphysical paintings AND Saturday morning cartoons' sense of wonder. Taste crosses every boundary. It takes what's alive wherever it finds it.

Taste is **the ability to distinguish what is genuinely resonant from what merely appears to be.** It is pattern recognition trained on a massive, diverse dataset of human creative achievement. The wider and deeper your intake, the more reliable your instinct becomes.

### The Three Components of Taste

**Discernment** — The ability to perceive quality and understand why something works or doesn't. This comes from consuming enormous amounts of creative work across every discipline and actively analyzing the *why* behind your emotional responses.

**Judgment** — The ability to choose between options when all seem viable. This is the hardest skill. It requires knowing not just what's good but what's *right for this project, this team, this moment*. A brilliant idea in the wrong context is a bad idea.

**Courage** — The willingness to commit to a direction that feels right even when it's unproven, uncomfortable, or commercially risky. Taste without courage produces safe work. Courage without taste produces self-indulgent work. Together, they produce art.

## 2. How Legendary Directors Found the Right Direction

### Fumito Ueda — Finding the Game Inside the Feeling

Ueda didn't start with mechanics or story. He started with a *feeling*: "I want the player to feel what it's like to hold someone's hand and be afraid to let go." That feeling became Ico. The entire game — the combat, the puzzles, the architecture, the companion AI — serves that single emotional truth. The reason Ico endures isn't its puzzle design or graphics. It's that the feeling is *specific* and *universal* simultaneously: everyone knows what it means to hold on to someone you're terrified of losing.

**Lesson:** The right direction often begins with an emotion so specific it seems too personal to be universal. Trust it. The more specific the feeling, the more universally it resonates — because specificity is what makes art feel *true* rather than *generic*.

### Hidetaka Miyazaki — Extracting Structure from Incomprehension

Miyazaki has spoken about reading English-language fantasy novels as a child without fully understanding the language. The gaps in comprehension forced his imagination to fill in the blanks — and the imagined versions were stranger, more evocative, and more personal than the originals could have been. This experience of *partial understanding generating creative completion* became the design philosophy of the Souls games: a world where the player receives fragments and must construct meaning.

**Lesson:** Limitation is a creative engine. Incompleteness, ambiguity, and gaps are not flaws to be fixed — they are spaces for the audience's imagination. The right direction sometimes means *removing* clarity rather than adding it. What you withhold shapes the experience as much as what you reveal.

### Hideo Kojima — Cross-Pollination as Creative Identity

Kojima's work is a collision of influences that no one else would have combined: Cold War espionage thrillers, postmodern philosophy, 1980s action cinema, Japanese mecha anime, European art films, and genetic science. Metal Gear Solid doesn't reference these influences — it *metabolizes* them into something that couldn't have come from any single tradition.

**Lesson:** The right direction is often found at the intersection of influences that "don't belong together." If your combination of inspirations makes sense to everyone immediately, it's probably too obvious. If it makes no sense to anyone, it's probably too arbitrary. The sweet spot: a combination that seems surprising until the player experiences it, and then seems *inevitable*.

### Jenova Chen — Designing for the Emotion the Medium Lacks

Chen observed that games excelled at adrenaline, competition, and power fantasy — and almost never attempted serenity, awe, or tenderness. He built Journey specifically to prove that games could produce the emotions they'd never tried. The right direction was defined by *what was missing*, not by what was popular.

**Lesson:** Survey the emotional landscape of existing games. Find the gaps. What feelings has the medium never seriously attempted? Compassion without combat? The boredom of waiting? The shame of complicity? The warmth of being recognized by a stranger? These gaps are opportunities. The right direction is often the emotion no one else is chasing.

### The Fullbright Company — The Game That Doesn't Need Combat

Gone Home was built around a creative director's conviction that exploring an empty house and piecing together a family's story through objects would be emotionally compelling without any traditional game mechanics — no enemies, no puzzles, no fail states. The industry said this wasn't a game. Players cried.

**Lesson:** The right direction sometimes means stripping away what the industry assumes is essential. Ask: what if we remove the mechanic everyone expects? What's left? If the answer is "nothing," your concept is built on mechanics, not meaning. If the answer is "the actual experience," you've found the core.

### Toby Fox — One Person's Obsession as Universal Truth

Undertale was made by essentially one person who poured a lifetime of influences (JRPGs, comedy, internet culture, genuine moral philosophy, the music of Shinji Okazaki) into a game that *shouldn't* have worked by any industry standard. It worked because the creator's obsession was authentic — every pixel reflected a genuine creative vision, not a market calculation.

**Lesson:** Authenticity of vision trumps production value. A game that is unmistakably the expression of *someone's* genuine creative obsession — even a rough, modest game — resonates more deeply than a polished game that was designed by committee to appeal to demographics.

## 3. The Anatomy of a Wrong Direction

Wrong directions share recognizable patterns. Learn to detect them early.

### "It's Like X But With Y"

If the concept can be fully described as a mashup of existing games, the direction is derivative. Comparison is useful for communication ("it's *reminiscent* of X") but dangerous as a foundation ("it *is* X + Y"). The right direction can be *contextualized* by comparisons but not *defined* by them.

### The Theme-Mechanic Divorce

The story is about one thing and the gameplay is about something completely different, with no intentional tension between them. The narrative says "war is hell" while the combat makes war thrilling. This isn't always wrong (some games deliberately explore this dissonance), but *unintentional* divorce is a direction problem. If you can't articulate what the mechanics and narrative share, the direction is split.

### The Tourism Problem

The world is beautiful but the player has no reason to care about it beyond aesthetic appreciation. This happens when world-building is prioritized over thematic grounding — when the designer builds a place to *visit* rather than a place to *inhabit*. A game world must make the player feel like a participant, not a tourist.

### The Cleverness Trap

The concept is intellectually interesting but emotionally empty. It's a good idea for an essay, not a game. Cleverness impresses briefly. Emotional truth persists. If a concept makes people say "that's clever" rather than "that made me feel something," the direction needs an emotional anchor.

### The Scope Mirage

The vision is so vast that no team could execute it — and the vastness *is* the appeal, rather than a specific, achievable core experience. "A procedurally generated universe with millions of planets" is a scope statement, not a direction. "The loneliness of standing on a planet where no one has ever stood" is a direction.

### The Market-Chasing Reflex

Building the game the market wants today rather than the game the world needs tomorrow. By the time a trend is identifiable, the games that defined it are already in production. The right direction creates demand rather than following it.

## 4. Developing Judgment Through Cross-Disciplinary Immersion

Taste develops through exposure. Here are structured practices:

### The Weekly Discipline Rotation

Each week, deeply engage with one discipline outside of games:
- **Week 1**: Visit a gallery or study a specific painter's body of work. Ask: how does this artist control my attention? What can I steal for environmental design?
- **Week 2**: Read poetry (not novels — poetry). Ask: how does this achieve emotional impact in so few words? What can I steal for dialogue and in-game text?
- **Week 3**: Listen to a complete album or symphony you've never heard, in a genre you don't know. Ask: how does this organize time? What can I steal for pacing?
- **Week 4**: Read history or anthropology. Ask: what human dynamics here are stranger and more compelling than anything in my current game world?

### The "Why Did This Move Me?" Journal

Every time a work of art — in any medium — produces a genuine emotional response, write three things:
1. **What happened.** The specific moment, image, line, or note.
2. **What I felt.** The specific emotion, as precisely as you can name it.
3. **Why it worked.** The structural reason — composition, timing, contrast, subtext, restraint, surprise.

Over time, this journal becomes your personal theory of emotional impact — the most valuable creative asset you own.

### The "Opposite Day" Exercise

When you notice you always reach for the same type of inspiration (always Japanese aesthetics, always grimdark, always melancholic), deliberately go to the opposite. If you gravitate toward dark, study joy. If you gravitate toward minimalism, study Baroque excess. If you gravitate toward European traditions, study African or Polynesian or pre-Columbian ones. Your taste grows fastest at its edges.

### The Conversation Practice

Talk to people who are not game designers about your game ideas. Not for validation — for translation. If you can't explain why your game matters to a poet, a chef, a nurse, or a historian, you don't yet understand what it's about at the human level. Non-game-industry people detect emotional authenticity and thematic clarity faster than industry people do, because they're not distracted by mechanics.

## 5. The Decision Framework: Choosing What to Build

When facing multiple viable directions, use this framework:

### The Intersection Test
Map your three strongest influences (from any discipline) and find where they intersect. The intersection is usually more original than any single influence alone. "What if a game felt like Tarkovsky's long takes + the ecological interconnection of a coral reef + the moral weight of Primo Levi's memoir?" — that intersection points somewhere no game has been.

### The Personal Stakes Test
Which direction would you be *devastated* not to make? Not "disappointed" — devastated. The project that keeps you up at night, that you sketch on napkins, that you bring up at dinner when no one asked — that's the direction with the energy to survive production.

### The "First 10 Minutes" Test
Imagine the first 10 minutes of each possible direction. Which one makes you feel something? Not "looks cool" — *feel something*. If the opening of the game doesn't produce emotion, the rest won't either, because the opening is where the creative instinct is strongest and the production compromises haven't yet accumulated.

### The Legacy Test
Fast forward 10 years. Which version of this game do you want to have made? Not which sold best — which one would you be proud to have your name on? Which one contributes something genuine to the medium? The answer to this question cuts through every other consideration.

## 6. Reading the Cultural Moment

Great games speak to their moment without being enslaved by it. Understand the cultural currents and decide whether to ride them, complicate them, or swim against them.

**What does the world need right now?** Not "what does the market want" but "what does the culture hunger for?" In times of alienation, games about connection resonate. In times of information overload, games about simplicity and presence resonate. In times of polarization, games about genuine moral complexity resonate. Feel the cultural temperature and respond to it — not with propaganda or preaching, but with experiences that address the emotional undercurrents.

**Counter-programming.** Sometimes the right direction is the opposite of what everyone else is doing. When the industry floods with multiplayer live-service games, a deeply personal single-player game stands out. When everything is dark and gritty, something genuinely hopeful cuts through. Counter-programming isn't contrarian — it's recognizing that the audience's appetite is shaped by what they *haven't* been getting.

**Timeless themes in contemporary skin.** The themes that resonate across centuries (identity, mortality, belonging, power, love, justice) can be expressed through contemporary anxieties (surveillance, AI, climate, displacement, digital identity). The combination of ancient theme and modern context produces work that feels both eternal and urgent.

## 7. The Courage to Commit

The hardest part of taste is not knowing the right direction — it's committing to it when it's unproven.

**Commit to the uncomfortable choice.** If two directions are viable and one is safer, the dangerous one is almost always more interesting. Safety in creative direction produces forgettable work. The games people remember 20 years later are the ones where the team said "I'm not sure this will work, but we have to try."

**Commit to depth over breadth.** It's better to explore one theme completely than to gesture at five themes superficially. A game that is *entirely* about one thing — the cost of knowledge, the weight of responsibility, the nature of memory — and explores it from every angle through every system, will resonate more deeply than a game that touches on many things and masters none.

**Commit to the version only you can make.** Whatever your unique combination of influences, experiences, and obsessions — that's your creative fingerprint. The game that only you (or only your team) would have made is, by definition, original. The game that anyone could have made is, by definition, derivative.

## 8. When to Change Direction

Not every commitment should be permanent. These are legitimate signals that the direction needs to change:

- **The team can't feel it.** If the people making the game don't believe in it after genuine effort, the direction might be right but the team is wrong — or (more often) the direction is intellectually sound but emotionally hollow.
- **The prototype disproves the thesis.** You believed the core mechanic would produce a specific feeling, and after prototyping, it doesn't. Trust the prototype over the pitch document.
- **The world changed.** A real-world event has made your game's premise tone-deaf, offensive, or trivial. Adapt.
- **You're solving the wrong problem.** Months into production, you realize the game is about something different than you thought — and the new "about" is more interesting. This is not failure. This is discovery. Have the courage to follow it.

Changing direction is not defeat. Refusing to change direction in the face of clear evidence *is*.

## 9. Taste in Practice: Micro-Decisions That Define a Game

Taste doesn't only operate at the concept level. It operates in every decision, every day:

**What color is the sky at the moment the character dies?** This is a taste decision. Orange-gold (warmth, sunset, peace) tells a different story than grey (emptiness, entropy) or clear blue (indifference of the universe, life continuing).

**What does the player hear when they open the menu?** A taste decision. Silence (the world pauses with you). Ambient sound continuing (the world doesn't wait). A specific musical motif (every menu visit is a tiny ritual).

**What word does this character use for "hello"?** A taste decision. "Hey" (casual, modern). "Greetings" (formal, distant). Nothing at all, just a nod (action over words). The character's name for the player (intimacy, recognition). Each choice ripples through every interaction.

**Which detail in this room tells the story?** A taste decision. The overturned chair says "struggle." The neatly made bed says "routine that continued until it couldn't." The child's drawing on the wall says "this place was once safe." You have space for one. Which one carries the most?

**When does the music stop?** A taste decision. Before the revelation (silence amplifies the twist). During the revelation (absence of music says "this is real"). After the revelation (the player sits in the emotional aftermath). Each choice creates a different relationship between the player and the moment.

These micro-decisions, accumulated across thousands of instances, are what give a game its texture, its feel, its identity. A game where every micro-decision was made with taste *feels* authored — coherent — intentional — alive. A game where these decisions were made by default or by habit feels generic, even if its macro-level concept is strong.

Taste is not a luxury. It is the difference between a game that is played and a game that is *felt*.
