## 1. Three-Act Structure in Games

The classic setup-confrontation-resolution, adapted for interactivity.

**Act 1 — The Tutorial World (10-15% of playtime)**
Establish the protagonist's ordinary world, teach core mechanics, and plant the inciting incident. The key game-specific insight: the player must *enjoy* the tutorial world enough to want to save it, but feel the *pull* of the unknown world beyond it.

Pattern: Introduce mechanics in a safe context → use those mechanics in a narrative-critical moment → the world breaks open.

Example approach: The player learns to climb in a peaceful village. Then they climb to escape when the village burns. The mechanic hasn't changed — the context has. Now climbing means survival.

**Act 2 — The Widening Gyre (60-70% of playtime)**
This is where games diverge most from film. Act 2 in a film is notoriously difficult because it's "the middle." In games, Act 2 *is* the game — it's where the core loop lives. Structure it as a series of escalating challenges, each of which reveals new facets of the world and characters.

Sub-structure for game Act 2:
- 2A: Exploration and alliance-building. The player gathers tools, companions, and knowledge. Rising competence matches rising narrative stakes.
- Midpoint reversal: A major revelation that recontextualizes everything. The mentor is the villain. The quest was a lie. The world is not what it seemed.
- 2B: Consequences and commitment. The player applies everything learned. Allies are tested. The cost of the journey becomes real.

**Act 3 — The Convergence (15-20% of playtime)**
All threads pull together. In games, Act 3 must do something film doesn't: make the player feel that *their* journey — not just the character's — is reaching its conclusion. The final challenge should test every skill the player has learned, and the narrative should acknowledge the player's specific path where possible.

## 2. Five-Act Structure

Borrowed from Shakespearean drama, this structure works exceptionally well for longer games and episodic content.

- **Act 1 — Exposition:** World and conflict established
- **Act 2 — Rising Action:** Complications multiply, allies and enemies crystallize
- **Act 3 — Climax / Turning Point:** The central confrontation or revelation (note: this is the *middle*, not the end)
- **Act 4 — Falling Action:** Consequences cascade, the protagonist must adapt to the new reality
- **Act 5 — Resolution:** Final confrontation and denouement

The power of five acts for games: Act 4 — the post-climax fallout — is where games can shine. After the big twist, give the player space to *live in* the changed world. Let them see how NPCs react, how the environment has shifted, how their choices ripple outward. This is the breathing room that makes the final act hit harder.

## 3. Kishotenketsu

A four-act structure from East Asian storytelling tradition that does not require conflict as its engine. Crucial for games that want to tell gentler stories.

- **Ki (Introduction):** Establish the world and characters
- **Shō (Development):** Deepen and expand, but without escalating conflict
- **Ten (Twist):** Introduce a surprising element that recontextualizes everything — not a *conflict*, but a *perspective shift*
- **Ketsu (Conclusion):** Reconcile the new understanding with the established world

This structure is visible in many Nintendo games. Individual levels in Mario often follow kishotenketsu: introduce a mechanic (Ki), develop it (Shō), combine it with something unexpected (Ten), then let the player demonstrate mastery of the combination (Ketsu).

For narrative games: Kishotenketsu is ideal for stories about discovery, wonder, relationship-building, and coming-of-age — stories where the "antagonist" is ignorance or distance, not a villain.

## 4. The Monomyth in Interactive Media

Campbell's Hero's Journey maps naturally to game progression, but beware the traps.

**What works:** The departure-initiation-return framework mirrors the player's own journey from novice to master. The "belly of the whale" moment (the point of no return) is powerful when tied to a permanent game state change. The "apotheosis" (moment of transcendence) should coincide with the player unlocking their final ability or reaching peak mechanical mastery.

**What doesn't work:** Slavish adherence. Not every game needs a mentor, a goddess, or a resurrection. The monomyth is a toolkit, not a checklist. Take the stages that serve your story and discard the rest.

**Game-specific addition — The Player's Monomyth:** The player themselves undergoes a journey: from confusion (new controls, unknown world) through competence (mastering systems) to transcendence (expressing creativity through mastery). The narrative monomyth and the player's monomyth should peak together.

## 5. Branching Narrative Architectures

### The Diamond
Branches diverge but always reconverge at key story nodes. This is the most practical architecture for AAA production — it gives the illusion of vast choice while keeping the critical path manageable.

### The Waterfall
Major choices cascade into increasingly divergent paths. Expensive to produce but yields genuinely different experiences. Best suited for shorter games or games where replayability is a core pillar.

### The Hub-and-Spoke
A central narrative hub (a town, a base, a relationship) with radiating quest lines that the player tackles in any order. Each spoke can have its own internal structure. The hub evolves based on which spokes have been completed.

### The Parallel Paths
Two or more largely separate storylines that the player can switch between or that converge at the climax. Effective for ensemble casts or faction-based narratives.

### The Web
Highly interconnected nodes where every choice subtly affects multiple other storylines. Extremely complex to author but creates the richest sense of consequence. Best supported by systemic/procedural tools rather than pure hand-authoring.

## 6. Modular / Non-Linear Structures

For open-world and sandbox games where player-directed pacing is essential.

**The Vignette Model:** Self-contained narrative modules that can be experienced in any order. Each is complete on its own but enriches the others. The overall narrative emerges from the *accumulation* of vignettes rather than from sequential progression.

**The Mystery Model:** The player pieces together a pre-existing story by discovering fragments out of order. The narrative is archaeological — digging into the past rather than driving the future. The player's interpretation becomes part of the story.

**The Systemic Model:** Narrative emerges from the interaction of game systems rather than from scripted events. Factions with goals, NPCs with schedules, ecosystems with dynamics — stories happen *to* the player rather than *for* the player. The writer's job shifts from authoring events to authoring *agents* and *systems* that produce events.

---

## 7. Emergent Narrative Frameworks

For games where stories arise from systems rather than scripts.

**Agent-based narratives:** Define NPCs with goals, relationships, and behavioral rules. Stories emerge from their interactions with each other and the player. The writer designs the *space of possible stories* rather than a single story.

**Procedural narrative generation:** Systems that assemble narrative beats from authored components based on game state. Requires a grammar of narrative (what can follow what, what combinations create drama, what constitutes a satisfying arc).

**Player-authored narrative:** Give players tools to create their own stories within the world. The designer's role is to create a setting rich enough to inspire and mechanics expressive enough to narrate.

## 8. Hybrid Models for Modern AAA

Most successful modern games blend multiple structures:

**Linear spine + modular flesh:** A main storyline with clear act structure, surrounded by modular side content that can be experienced in any order. The spine provides momentum; the modules provide depth.

**Gated open world:** Open-world exploration within defined narrative zones. Completing zone objectives advances the global narrative and unlocks new zones. Each zone has its own internal narrative structure.

**Living narrative:** A base narrative that evolves through post-launch content (seasons, expansions, live events). Requires a narrative architecture designed for extensibility — a world with unanswered questions, unresolved tensions, and unexplored regions.
